  - id: executive-summary
    title: "Executive Summary"
    summary: "The inaugural 2023 Design Pay Asia report captures the state of design careers across Southeast Asia based on 323 survey responses, with a landmark finding of a 42% gender pay gap in Singapore."
    body: |
      The 2023 Design Pay Asia survey, conducted between December 2022 and February 2023, captured 323 valid responses from designers working across Southeast Asia. This is the inaugural edition of what would become an annual benchmark for design compensation and careers in the region.

      The data reveals a young, diverse workforce concentrated primarily in Singapore (69.4%), with growing representation from Malaysia (14.2%) and Indonesia (8.9%). The gender distribution was nearly equal — a notable achievement for a tech-adjacent survey — with women at 54.5% and men at 43.9%.

      Product design dominates as the primary field at 67.5% of roles, and individual contributors make up 87% of respondents. The workforce skews toward early-to-mid career, with nearly half having six or fewer years of design experience, and a significant proportion being career switchers with more total work experience than design-specific experience.

      **The headline finding of this report is a 42% gender pay gap in Singapore.** Women earn substantially less than men across nearly all career levels, feel less fairly compensated, report lower negotiation confidence, and have slower career levelling progress. This finding galvanised the community and set the agenda for subsequent DPA research.

      Compensation in Singapore shows clear progression with seniority: Junior ICs earn a median of S$54,000, rising to S$238,900 at Director level. The average annual salary growth per year of design experience is S$10,839. International experience commands an 85.5% premium, and remote work offers over 2.7x higher compensation than in-office roles.

      Hybrid work is the established norm at 71.8%, large enterprises and startups are the top employers, and banking/capital markets alongside consumer products are the best-paying industries.

      This report provides a foundational benchmark for design compensation in Southeast Asia and established the evidence base that would drive DPA's advocacy for pay transparency, negotiation empowerment, and gender equity in the years that followed.

  - id: demographics
    title: "1. Demographics"
    summary: "A young, diverse workforce concentrated in Singapore, with near-equal gender representation and a strong urban skew."
    body: |
      ## Geographic Distribution

      The inaugural 2023 survey reached designers across 10 countries, with a strong concentration in three key markets:

      - **Singapore: 224 respondents (69.4%)** — the dominant market
      - **Malaysia: 46 respondents (14.2%)** — the second-largest cohort
      - **Indonesia: 29 respondents (8.9%)** — an emerging presence
      - **Philippines: 8 respondents (2.5%)**
      - **Vietnam: 7 respondents (2.2%)**
      - **Thailand, India: 3 each**
      - **Taiwan, Romania, Japan: 1 each**

      While Singapore represents the majority of responses, the survey established beachheads in neighbouring markets that would grow significantly in subsequent years. The concentration in Singapore reflects both the maturity of its design community and the survey's initial distribution channels.

      ## Age Profile

      The workforce skews young: the majority of respondents were under 35. The 25-30 age bracket is the largest at 43%, forming the core of the design workforce. Across all age groups under 35, women outnumbered men. Above age 36, the gender pattern shifts, with men dominating each older age bracket.

      Age distribution: 18-24 (16.4%), 25-30 (43.0%), 31-35 (25.4%), 36-40 (9.9%), 41-45 (3.1%), 46-50 (1.5%).

      ## Gender Distribution

      The 2023 survey achieved a nearly equal gender split: **54.5% women (176), 43.9% men (142)**, with small numbers of non-binary (2) and prefer-not-to-state (2) respondents, and one transgender man. This gender balance was a deliberate outcome of distribution strategy and community partnerships — and it proved critical for the gender pay gap analysis that became the report's most impactful finding.

      The over-35 shift toward male dominance suggests structural retention challenges for women in design careers, a pattern that would be explored more deeply in subsequent reports.

  - id: roles-and-experience
    title: "2. Roles and Experience"
    summary: "Product design dominates, individual contributors are the norm, and many designers are career switchers with more total work experience than design-specific experience."
    body: |
      ## Job Titles

      Designers reported a wide variety of titles, but nearly 90% included the word "design." Over half (50.7%) featured "UX" and 25.3% had "UI" in the title. The most common titles were Product Designer (33), UX Designer (25), UI/UX Designer (18), and Senior Product Designer (18).

      Around 40.8% of job titles indicated some level of seniority, suggesting that formal levelling is common but not universal in the region's design industry.

      ## Career Level Distribution

      Individual contributors dominate, making up 87% of all respondents:

      - **Junior IC: 101 (31.3%)** — the largest single cohort
      - **Mid-level IC: 76 (23.5%)**
      - **Senior IC: 72 (22.3%)**
      - **Lead / Staff / Principal IC: 32 (9.9%)**
      - **People Manager: 27 (8.4%)**
      - **Director: 11 (3.4%)**
      - **Vice President: 4 (1.2%)**

      ## Primary Field of Work

      Product design overwhelmingly dominates at 67.5% of respondents (218), followed by Research (24, 7.4%), Design Leadership & Management (22, 6.8%), Graphic/Marketing Design (15, 4.6%), Content Design/Writing (13, 4.0%), and Design Operations (8, 2.5%).

      ## Experience and Career Switching

      Nearly half of respondents (48.4%) reported six or fewer years of design experience, with the single largest group (22.9%) having just one year. However, the median total working experience (10 years) is notably higher than the median design experience (6 years), revealing that many designers are career switchers who bring broader professional experience to their design roles.

      ## Education

      The workforce is highly educated: 66.3% hold bachelor's degrees, 15.5% have master's degrees, and 13.6% have diplomas. Formal design education is the most common path (49.8%), followed by online certification (29.1%) and bootcamps (27.9%). Notably, 22.3% have no formal design education at all — demonstrating that design education is not a barrier to employment in the field.

      ## International Experience

      28.5% of respondents have lived and worked in another country. This international exposure would prove to be one of the strongest predictors of higher compensation, commanding an 85.5% premium over locally-only careers.

  - id: compensation
    title: "3. Compensation"
    summary: "Local currency dominates, median compensation varies across markets, and annual bonuses remain a significant part of total pay packages."
    body: |
      ## Currency and Payment

      Designers across Southeast Asia are almost universally paid in local currency. Singapore Dollar dominates at 223 respondents, followed by Malaysian Ringgit (46), Indonesian Rupiah (27), Philippine Peso (7), Vietnamese Dong (6), and Thai Baht (3). Only a small handful receive USD or other foreign currencies, suggesting limited remote work for overseas employers at this stage.

      ## Median Annual Total Compensation

      **Singapore: S$84,000 (N=223)** — High confidence across all career levels. The full range spans from S$12,000 at Junior IC to S$355,000 at Director level. Compensation by level shows steady progression: Junior IC S$54,000, Mid-level IC S$75,800, Senior IC S$119,500, Lead/Staff IC S$103,200, People Manager S$139,100, Director S$238,900, VP S$176,500.

      **Malaysia: RM83,000 (N=46)** — Medium confidence for Junior to Senior ICs. Malaysia has the second-largest respondent count but trails Singapore significantly, limiting analysis depth at senior levels.

      **Indonesia: Rp84,000,000 (N=27)** — Medium confidence for Junior ICs only. Indonesia's smaller sample means that salary trends beyond the junior level should be interpreted with caution. Junior IC median: Rp62,000,000, Mid-level IC: Rp84,000,000, Senior IC: Rp348,000,000.

      ## Compensation Mix

      Annual bonuses remain a significant part of total compensation, with 54.8% of respondents receiving them. 30.3% receive other annual compensation, and 15.8% receive annual stock grants. Stock-based compensation is notably less common than in Western markets, reflecting the regional prevalence of local companies and enterprises over venture-funded startups.

      ## Standard Benefits

      The five standard non-pay benefits that form the baseline package for designers in the region:

      1. **Paid medical leave: 83.3%**
      2. **Paid time off / vacation time: 77.4%**
      3. **Health insurance: 76.5%**
      4. **Flexible working arrangement: 68.7%**
      5. **Budget for equipment or home office setup: 68.7%**

      Additional common benefits include professional development funds (46.4%), dental insurance (39.3%), flexible schedule (39.9%), and onsite food/snacks (33.4%).

  - id: company-landscape
    title: "4. Company Landscape"
    summary: "Large enterprises and startups are the top employers, hybrid work is the new norm, and remote roles command a significant compensation premium."
    body: |
      ## Company Type

      Large enterprises (multinational corporations) are the top destination at 32.2%, followed closely by startups at 28.5%. Agency and consulting firms account for 20.4%, with SMEs making up the remainder. Government (3.4%), non-profits (0.9%), and academia (0.6%) represent smaller but stable employer categories.

      ## Industry Distribution

      Two industries stand out as the primary magnets for design talent:

      - **Banking & Capital Markets: 20.1%** — the top-paying sector
      - **Consumer Products: 19.8%** — close second in both share and pay

      Other significant industries include Transportation/Hospitality & Services, Government & Public Services, Investment Management, Retail/Wholesale & Distribution, Health Care, and Insurance. Life Sciences pays the highest median salary but has sparse data points, while Automotive and Healthcare offer the lowest pay.

      ## Company and Team Size

      Designers are evenly spread across company sizes, except for very small operations (1-10 employees). The most common design team size is 2-10 people (54.2%), followed by 11-50 people (27.6%). Solo designers (team of 1) make up 7.4%, while large teams of 200+ account for just 2.5%.

      Median pay tends to increase with company size, with a notable 25.5% jump between company sizes of 51-200 and 201-1,000 employees.

      ## Local vs International Companies

      The majority of designers (66.2%) work at their company's headquarters, suggesting most are employed by local or locally-headquartered companies. However, working at a non-local company offers a significant 67.1% higher median pay, with global companies paying even more — up to 91.5% more for designers without formal design education.

      ## Work Arrangement

      **Hybrid work is the established norm at 71.8% (232 respondents)**. Fully remote accounts for 16.7% (54), and in-office is a minority at 11.5% (37). This distribution reflects the post-pandemic shift toward flexible working that had already become entrenched by early 2023.

      The compensation implications are stark: **fully remote roles offer over 2.7x higher median pay than in-office roles** and 1.8x higher than hybrid. This premium reflects the access remote work provides to global and non-local employers with higher salary bands.

      ## Work Hours

      Most designers work extra hours: 32.8% work none beyond standard, 26.9% work 1-2 extra hours per week, 20.1% work 3-5 extra hours, and 16.4% work more than 5 extra hours weekly. Directors and VPs work the most prolonged hours, while younger designers tend to have more balanced lifestyles.

  - id: gender-pay-gap
    title: "5. The Gender Pay Gap"
    summary: "The headline finding: women earn 42% less than men in Singapore's design industry. The gap persists across nearly all levels, and is compounded by differences in negotiation, career progression, and sentiment."
    body: |
      ## The 42% Gap

      The most significant finding of the 2023 report is a **42% gender pay gap in Singapore's design industry**. The median total compensation for women is 42% lower than men's, a gap that reflects multiple compounding factors.

      Underpinning this headline figure: men's average years of design experience (7.47 years) is nearly double that of women (3.86 years). This experience differential contributes to the pay gap but does not fully explain it — the gap persists within experience bands and across most career levels.

      ## The Gap by Career Level

      Across the Individual Contributor track, the pay gap favours men at most levels, with women earning **11-21% less**. The exception is the Junior IC level, where women actually earn 11% more than men — suggesting that pay disparities emerge and widen as careers progress rather than being present from the start.

      On the Management track, pay gaps also favour men. Men are over-represented in senior roles with many more years of experience accumulated, and this experience premium translates directly into significantly higher compensation.

      At the Director level, there are too few data points to draw conclusions — reflecting the underrepresentation of women at the most senior design levels.

      *Methodology note: the per-level pay gap figures (Mid IC, Senior IC, Management) are estimates derived from unpublished per-level breakdowns and should be read as indicative. The published evidence base supports the overall 42% gap, the Junior IC direction (women earn more), and an IC-track range of 11-21%; individual level figures carry wider uncertainty due to small sub-group sizes at some career levels.*

      ## Beyond Pay: Sentiment and Behaviour

      The pay gap is compounded by differences in behaviour and perception:

      - **Negotiation:** 61% of men negotiated their current compensation vs 51% of women
      - **Fair compensation perception:** Men feel more fairly compensated for their role, level, and effort
      - **Market awareness:** Men report higher awareness of market rates
      - **Negotiation confidence:** Men feel more confident and informed to negotiate their next offer
      - **Career progression:** 62% of men made formal job levelling progress in the past year vs 52% of women
      - **Job seeking:** Women are slightly more likely to actively seek new jobs in the coming year

      ## Pay Increase Patterns

      An interesting dynamic emerges in pay increase outcomes. More men increased their pay in the 0.1-5% and 11-15% brackets, while women performed better in the other brackets (more than 20%, 16-20%, and 6-10%). However, a slightly higher percentage of women reported no pay increase at all compared to men. This polarisation — women leading both the highest and lowest increase brackets — suggests that outcomes for women are less predictable and more volatile than for men.

      ## Implications

      The gender pay gap findings set the agenda for DPA's advocacy work in subsequent years, driving research into pay transparency, negotiation empowerment programmes, and industry-wide conversations about structural barriers to equity in design careers. The 2023 report established that the gap is real, measurable, and multi-dimensional — not simply a function of experience or role differences, but a systemic issue requiring structural solutions.

  - id: what-drives-higher-pay
    title: "6. What Drives Higher Pay"
    summary: "Experience matters more than education. International experience, remote work, and company type are the strongest compensation multipliers."
    body: |
      ## Experience: The Primary Driver

      Salaries grow steadily with experience. Based on Singapore data, the average annual salary increase designers can expect as they gain more years of experience is **S$10,839 per year**. This steady growth rewards career persistence and deepening expertise.

      Management track roles pay more overall, with more experienced designers filling those positions. The progression from Junior IC (S$54,000 median) to Director (S$238,900 median) represents a 4.4x multiple over the course of a career.

      Negotiation matters more at mid-to-late career stages. For Junior to Senior ICs, the median pay difference between those who negotiate and those who don't is minimal. But for leads and managers, negotiation yields a **10-16% increase**. The impact is even more pronounced for designers with 7+ years of experience, who see **11-22% more** when they negotiate.

      ## International Experience: 85% Premium

      Designers with overseas working experience earn **85.5% higher median salaries** than those without. The minimum salary for internationally-experienced designers is 190% greater, and they accumulate more years of design experience on average (7.1 vs 4.4 years for locals).

      This premium reflects both the value of international exposure and the tendency for designers with overseas experience to work at global or non-local companies, which themselves pay 67.1% more than local firms.

      ## Remote Work: 2.7x Premium

      Fully remote roles offer the highest compensation premium: **over 2.7x higher than in-office roles** and 1.8x higher than hybrid. This reflects the access remote work provides to global employers with higher salary bands, as well as the tendency for remote roles to be concentrated at more senior levels.

      ## Company and Industry Factors

      **Company type:** Large enterprises offer the highest median pay, followed by startups. Agencies and consultancies provide earlier career opportunities. Non-local companies consistently pay more than local ones.

      **Industry:** Banking & Capital Markets and Consumer Products are the top-paying sectors, compensating the experience levels required well. Life Sciences pays the highest median but has sparse data points. Government roles provide earlier career opportunities for less experienced designers.

      **Company size:** There is a 25.5% jump in median pay between company sizes of 51-200 and 201-1,000. Beyond that, differences are less significant.

      **Design team size:** Larger design teams correlate with higher earnings potential. Solo practitioners have the lowest median pay but require the least experience. Teams of 200+ have the highest median salary and the most experienced designers.

      ## Education: Experience Trumps Credentials

      Design education may not be the primary driver of salary outcomes. Those with no formal design education earn **23.6% more** than formally design-trained peers, despite having slightly less design experience. This counterintuitive finding is explained by the fact that non-traditional designers tend to be older, highly educated career switchers who bring broader professional experience valued by employers.

      90.2% of designers without design education hold non-design tertiary qualifications. Bachelor's degree holders earn a median of S$82,400, and Master's holders earn S$102,000 — tertiary education matters, but it doesn't need to be design-specific.

      In-person certification correlates to 20% higher pay than having no design education, while bootcamp graduates trend at the lower end of median pay (though they are also the youngest group).

      Larger companies and certain industries (particularly Consumer Products) appear more accommodating of non-traditional hires, suggesting that some employers value broader experience and transferable skills over design-specific credentials.

  - id: career-sentiment
    title: "7. Career Progression and Sentiment"
    summary: "Most designers have made career progress, but support from employers is mixed and many are actively seeking new opportunities."
    body: |
      ## Career Levelling Progress

      Over half of respondents (55.7%) reported making formal progress in job levelling in the past year. However, a substantial minority (30.7%) had not made progress, and 13.5% indicated the question was not applicable — pointing to potential challenges and obstacles in career advancement.

      ## Pay Increases

      Most respondents received pay increases compared to the previous year. 31.8% increased their pay by more than 15% (likely driven by job changes), while 21.3% received no increase at all — effectively a pay cut when accounting for inflation.

      ## Company Support for Growth

      Sentiment around company support for career growth was moderate, averaging **4.54 out of 7**. About half of designers felt their companies were supportive (scoring 5-6), but a significant number disagreed or felt ambivalent. This suggests uneven organisational investment in design career development.

      ## Learning and Development

      Designers showed moderate interest in exploring different disciplines and picking up new skills, averaging **4.34 out of 7**. However, there was less appetite for switching career tracks entirely (from IC to management or vice versa), with an average of **3.46 out of 7** — most designers are comfortable staying in their current track.

      ## Job Search Activity

      Over half of respondents were open to switching jobs or changing companies in the coming year, averaging **4.34 out of 7** on job-seeking intent. Notably, 21% strongly agreed (rating 7) that they were actively looking. This high level of openness to movement reflects both the dynamism of the design job market and potential dissatisfaction with current roles.

      ## Work Arrangement Comfort

      Despite the career uncertainties, designers are overwhelmingly comfortable with their current working arrangement, averaging **5.57 out of 7** — the highest sentiment score across all categories. 31.6% gave the maximum rating of 7. This suggests that hybrid and remote work models, once adopted, have become a valued and stable part of the design work experience.

      ## Working Hours

      67.2% of designers work extra hours beyond the standard work week, with 16.4% working more than 5 extra hours weekly. This translates to roughly 10.8 additional working days per year for the highest-intensity group. Senior roles (Directors and VPs) work the most prolonged hours, while younger designers tend to maintain more balanced lifestyles.

  - id: methodology
    title: "8. Methodology"
    summary: "Survey design, data collection, and known limitations of the inaugural 2023 DPA survey."
    body: |
      ## Survey Design

      The 2023 Design Pay Asia survey was an anonymous, voluntary online survey designed to capture compensation, career, and sentiment data from design professionals across Southeast Asia. The survey was conducted between December 2022 and February 2023 using Tally.so as the data collection platform.

      ## Distribution

      The survey was distributed through:
      - Design community channels and online forums
      - Partnerships with design organisations and communities in Singapore, Malaysia, and Indonesia
      - Social media and professional networks (LinkedIn, Twitter, Instagram)
      - Direct outreach to design leaders and teams across the region

      ## Data Collection and Cleaning

      A total of 323 valid responses were retained after data cleaning. Cleaning procedures included:
      - Removal of duplicate submissions
      - Exclusion of clearly invalid or joke responses
      - Verification of geographic consistency
      - Sanity checks on compensation figures
      - Filtering of incomplete responses below minimum threshold

      ## Known Limitations and Biases

      The survey methodology carries several inherent biases that should be considered when interpreting results:

      **Geographic skew:** Singapore represents 69.4% of responses, which means aggregate statistics are heavily weighted toward the Singapore market. Country-level breakdowns are provided where possible but should be interpreted with appropriate caution for smaller markets. Malaysia (N=46) and Indonesia (N=29) have limited sample sizes.

      **Community reach:** Responses were primarily collected through design community channels, which may over-represent designers who are active in professional networks and under-represent those working in isolation or outside major urban centres.

      **Language:** The survey was conducted in English, which may limit participation from designers who are less comfortable with English, particularly in Indonesia, Vietnam, and Thailand.

      **Digital access:** The online-only format excludes designers without reliable internet access, which may under-represent those in remote areas or from lower-income backgrounds.

      **Self-reported data:** All figures are self-reported and have not been independently verified. Compensation data in particular may be subject to recall bias, rounding, or currency conversion errors.

      **First-year limitations:** As an inaugural survey, the 2023 report has limited ability to identify trends over time. Comparisons with subsequent years should account for changes in methodology, distribution channels, and respondent demographics.

      **Small-cohort sensitivity:** Country-level data for markets with fewer than 30 respondents (Indonesia, Philippines, Vietnam, Thailand, and others) should be treated as indicative rather than statistically robust.

      ## Singapore Focus

      The detailed analysis sections of this report (gender pay gap, experience-salary correlation, education impact, company analysis) focus on Singapore respondents (N=223) due to the larger sample size. These findings may not generalise to other Southeast Asian markets.

# 2023 Design Pay Asia Report

## Southeast Asia's First Comprehensive Design Industry Survey

The inaugural 2023 Design Pay Asia survey captured 323 responses from designers across Southeast Asia. This report provides foundational data on compensation, career progression, company profiles, and workplace sentiment — and a landmark analysis revealing a 42% gender pay gap in Singapore's design industry.

---

## Executive Summary

The 2023 survey, conducted between December 2022 and February 2023, captured 323 responses from designers across 10 countries, with Singapore contributing 69.4% of the sample. The data reveals a young, diverse workforce: gender is nearly equal, 87% are individual contributors, and product design dominates at 67.5% of roles.

Key findings from the report:

1. **A 42% gender pay gap in Singapore.** Women earn substantially less than men across nearly all career levels. Men have nearly double the years of design experience, negotiate more often, feel more fairly compensated, and report faster career progression. This finding set the agenda for DPA's advocacy work.

2. **Experience drives pay.** Salaries grow at S$10,839 per year of design experience. Career switchers with broader work experience often out-earn traditionally-trained designers. Experience matters more than education.

3. **International experience commands an 85.5% premium.** Designers who have worked abroad earn significantly more, work at higher-paying companies, and progress faster in their careers.

4. **Remote work pays.** Fully remote roles offer over 2.7x higher median compensation than in-office positions, reflecting access to global employers with higher salary bands.

5. **Hybrid work is the norm at 71.8%**, with large enterprises and startups as the top employers, and banking/capital markets alongside consumer products as the best-paying industries.

6. **Career switchers thrive.** With median total work experience of 10 years versus median design experience of 6 years, many designers bring prior professional experience that employers value — sometimes more than design-specific credentials.

This report established the evidence base for DPA's mission: empowering designers with data to negotiate fairly, benchmark their careers, and advocate for equity in the design industry.
